# Writing Patterns

## Originality

Use only general craft patterns: ensemble kids, friendship-driven investigation, adult secrets, cross-cut suspense, period objects, and chapter-end hooks.

Do not copy existing characters, monsters, labs, dialogue, signature scenes, or plot sequences from any named work.

## Tone

Blend fast youth adventure with folk unease. Let dread come from familiar county-town things behaving wrong.

Use concrete 1990s details: BP machines, tape recorders, black-and-white TV, bicycles, video halls, enamel cups, county radio, summer homework, cheap flashlights, and damp archives.

## Scene Formula

Start with a visible problem.

Let the children act, argue, and discover.

Thread in one local detail with plot function.

Reveal one answer and one worse question.

End on a physical hook: a photo, voice, object, light, wrong name, reversed sign, or impossible person.

## Character Pressure

Each child needs a personal reason to enter danger:

林照野 wants proof and dignity for his father.

沈星河 wants the truth about her father without destroying her mother.

马小满 wants to be brave enough to keep his friends.

顾南舟 wants his brother back but must learn that return can be false.

阿铃 wants to finish an old duty and decide where she belongs.

## Mystery Design

Every supernatural clue should have a mundane surface explanation, then a stranger second meaning.

Avoid exposition dumps. Put history inside archives, songs, damaged photos, local gossip, stage props, and adult evasions.

Make the most dangerous offer emotionally tempting: the return of someone lost.
